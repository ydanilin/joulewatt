Problem 1.1.5.

Problem text:

Assuming that in an ideal Si diode \(e^{v_D/\eta V_t} \gg 1\). Than:
  • (a) what is the change in vD that multiplies iD by a factor k?
  • (b) what is the change in vD that doubles iD assuming η = 2?
  • (c) what is the change in vD that multiplies iD by a factor of 10 assuming:
    • (i) η = 2, and
    • (ii) η = 1.
  • (d) If ΔvD is the change in vD that causes a 10-fold change in iD, the diode is referred to as a ΔvD ∕ decade diode. What is η for a 0.1 V ∕ decade diode?

Solution:

(a):
As we know from P 1.1.2, very often it is worth to drop off "1" in the Main Diode Formula. To derive the k ratio, let's construct a quotient
\[\frac{{k{i_S}}}{{{i_S}}} = \frac{{{I_S}{e^{{\raise0.7ex\hbox{${{v_D} + \Delta v}$} \!\mathord{\left/ {\vphantom {{{v_D} + \Delta v} {n{V_T}}}}\right.} \!\lower0.7ex\hbox{${n{V_T}}$}}}}}}{{{I_S}{e^{{\raise0.7ex\hbox{${{v_D}}$} \!\mathord{\left/ {\vphantom {{{v_D}} {n{V_T}}}}\right.} \!\lower0.7ex\hbox{${n{V_T}}$}}}}}} \hspace{1cm} \Rightarrow \hspace{1cm} k = e\frac{{{v_D} + \Delta v - {v_D}}}{{n{V_T}}}\]
from which we can easily derive
\[\Delta v = \eta V_T \ln k\]
Note that than \(e^{v_D/\eta V_t} \gg 1\), Δv values do not depend on diode current.
(b), (c):
Now we can calculate the required voltage changes:
k η = 2 η = 1
2 0.036 0.018
10 0.120 0.060
You can see that at η = 1 voltage changes are shorter, which means current increases more rapidly. So, the lesser η value, the steeper the I-V curve.
(d):
A decade is a multiplier-by-10. Suppose we have a value of 0.1. The 1st decade will be 0.1×10 = 1, 2nd decade - 0.1×10×10 = 10, 3rd decade - 0.1×10×10×10 = 100 and so one. Simply put, this is a factor of 10n.
Thus, for 0.1V per decade diode ΔV = 0.1, k = 10. Based on the formula above, η = 1.67.
